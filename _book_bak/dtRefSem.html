30  Reference semantics

Many users new to programming may not have encountered reference semantics before.

This chapter briefly demonstrates how data.table objecs are edited in-place.

library(data.table)

30.1 Get object’s location in memory with address()

When you add a new column to an existing data.frame, the data.frame is copied behind the scenes - you can tell becasue its memory address (where it’s physically stored in your computer) changes:

df1 <- data.frame(alpha = 1:5, beta = 11:15)
address(df1)
[1] "0x106a13a48"
df1$gamma <- df1$alpha + df1$beta
address(df1)
[1] "0x106a9e578"

When you add a new column in a data.table in-place, the data.table object’s address remains unchanged:

dt1 <- data.table(alpha = 1:5, beta = 11:15)
address(dt1)
[1] "0x1043cf800"
dt1[, gamma := alpha + beta]
address(dt1)
[1] "0x1043cf800"

30.1.1 Reference semantics at work

Up to now, you are likely used to working with regular R objects that behave like this:

df1 <- data.frame(a = rep(1, 5))
df1
  a
1 1
2 1
3 1
4 1
5 1
df2 <- df1
df2
  a
1 1
2 1
3 1
4 1
5 1
df2$a <- df2$a*2
df2
  a
1 2
2 2
3 2
4 2
5 2
df1
  a
1 1
2 1
3 1
4 1
5 1
address(df1)
[1] "0x1138bf0b0"
address(df2)
[1] "0x113ad92a8"

data.table uses “reference semantics” or “pass-by-reference”. Be careful or you might get confused:

dt1 <- data.table(a = rep(1, 5))
dt1
   a
1: 1
2: 1
3: 1
4: 1
5: 1
dt2 <- dt1
dt2
   a
1: 1
2: 1
3: 1
4: 1
5: 1
dt2[, a := a * 2]
dt2
   a
1: 2
2: 2
3: 2
4: 2
5: 2
dt1
   a
1: 2
2: 2
3: 2
4: 2
5: 2
address(dt1)
[1] "0x1050d0600"
address(dt2)
[1] "0x1050d0600"

If you want to create a copy of a data.table, use copy(): 

dt3 <- copy(dt1)
dt3
   a
1: 2
2: 2
3: 2
4: 2
5: 2
address(dt3)
[1] "0x103118200"
dt3[, a := a * 2]
dt3
   a
1: 4
2: 4
3: 4
4: 4
5: 4
dt1
   a
1: 2
2: 2
3: 2
4: 2
5: 2
